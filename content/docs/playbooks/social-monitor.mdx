# Social Media Monitoring

Social media never sleeps, and neither should your monitoring. Whether someone is praising your product on Twitter, complaining about a bug on Reddit, or your biggest competitor just announced a new feature, you want to know about it. But spending hours scrolling through feeds and search results is not a productive use of your time.

The Social Media Monitoring playbook turns OpenClaw into your always-on social listening tool. It watches the platforms and keywords you care about, filters out the noise, and delivers actionable alerts when something needs your attention. Think of it as a radar system for your brand's online presence.

## What Gets Monitored

You can configure OpenClaw to watch for several types of social activity:

- **Brand mentions** — Any time someone mentions your company, product, or brand name
- **Keyword tracking** — Industry terms, competitor names, or topics relevant to your business
- **Sentiment shifts** — Sudden changes in how people talk about you (spikes in complaints, viral positive feedback)
- **Competitor activity** — New posts, announcements, or campaigns from competitors you are watching
- **Influencer engagement** — When notable people in your industry discuss topics you care about
- **Community activity** — Conversations in specific subreddits, Discord servers, or forums

The monitoring covers Twitter/X, LinkedIn, Reddit, Hacker News, and any RSS feeds you configure. Each platform gets checked at different intervals depending on how fast things move there.

## Why Not Just Use a Social Listening Tool

You might already have a tool like Mention, Brandwatch, or Sprout Social. Those are great products, and this playbook actually works well alongside them. The difference is that OpenClaw does not just collect mentions. It reads them, understands context, and tells you what matters and why.

A traditional social listening tool might show you 47 mentions today. OpenClaw tells you that 44 of them are routine, two are support issues that need responses, and one is a viral thread you should jump into while it is still gaining traction. It is the difference between data and insight.

## Deciding What to Track

Start narrow and expand over time. If you try to monitor everything from day one, you will drown in notifications. Here is a good starting sequence:

1. **Week 1**: Your brand name and product name
2. **Week 2**: Add your top two or three competitors
3. **Week 3**: Add industry keywords and hashtags
4. **Week 4**: Add influencer tracking and community monitoring

At each stage, tune the sensitivity. If you are getting too many irrelevant alerts, tighten your filters. If you are missing things, loosen them. OpenClaw learns from your feedback, so telling it "this alert was not useful" helps it filter better over time.

## SOUL.md Additions

```markdown
## Behaviors

### Social Media Monitoring
- Monitor configured platforms for brand mentions, keywords, and competitor activity
- Classify each mention by urgency: Urgent (needs response within 1 hour), Notable (worth seeing today), and Routine (include in daily digest)
- Urgent triggers: negative sentiment with high engagement, direct support requests, viral content mentioning us, competitor announcements
- For each alert, include: platform, author, content summary, engagement metrics, sentiment, and suggested response
- Never respond to social media posts on my behalf — always present options for me to choose from
- Daily digest at end of business with a summary of all activity, trends, and notable mentions
- Weekly trend report showing sentiment over time, top topics, and competitor comparison
- If a mention is gaining traction quickly (engagement doubling within an hour), escalate immediately regardless of scheduled check times
- Track sentiment trends — alert me if overall sentiment drops below the 30-day average
```

## Cron Configuration

```yaml
cron:
  social-scan-twitter:
    schedule: "*/15 * * * *"  # Every 15 minutes, 24/7
    timezone: "UTC"
    action: run-playbook
    playbook: social-scan
    config:
      platform: twitter
      keywords: ["your-brand", "your-product", "@your-handle"]
      competitor_handles: ["@competitor1", "@competitor2"]
      min_engagement: 5  # ignore tweets with fewer than 5 interactions
    retry:
      attempts: 1
      delay: 5m

  social-scan-reddit:
    schedule: "*/30 * * * *"  # Every 30 minutes
    timezone: "UTC"
    action: run-playbook
    playbook: social-scan
    config:
      platform: reddit
      subreddits: ["your-industry", "your-niche", "startups"]
      keywords: ["your-brand", "your-product"]
      min_upvotes: 3

  social-daily-digest:
    schedule: "0 18 * * 1-5"  # 6 PM weekdays
    timezone: "America/New_York"
    action: run-playbook
    playbook: social-daily-summary
    config:
      include_sentiment_chart: true
      include_top_mentions: 10

  social-weekly-report:
    schedule: "0 9 * * 1"  # Monday at 9 AM
    timezone: "America/New_York"
    action: run-playbook
    playbook: social-weekly-report
    config:
      compare_to_previous_week: true
      include_competitor_analysis: true
```

## Tips for Effective Monitoring

- **Set escalation paths**: Not everyone on your team should get urgent alerts. Route support-related mentions to your support team and competitive intelligence to your product team.
- **Track response time**: OpenClaw can measure how quickly you respond to urgent mentions and report on your average response time. Fast responses to public complaints dramatically improve brand perception.
- **Seasonal adjustments**: If you know a product launch, conference, or campaign is coming, temporarily increase monitoring frequency and lower the alert threshold so you catch everything during high-activity periods.
- **Sentiment baselines**: Do not overreact to a single bad day. Look at weekly and monthly trends. If sentiment has been consistently declining, that is a real signal. If it dips for one day, it is probably just one loud complaint.
- **Competitor playbook**: Create a separate SOUL.md section for competitive monitoring with specific things you want to track, like pricing changes, feature launches, or executive hires.
