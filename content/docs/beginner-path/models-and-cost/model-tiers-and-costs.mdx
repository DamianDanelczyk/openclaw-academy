Picking an AI model feels overwhelming at first. There are so many options, weird names, and version numbers that mean nothing to a newcomer. The good news is that for personal use, almost everything you'll ever need falls into just three tiers — and once you understand them, choosing becomes easy.

## The Three Tiers

Think of AI models like cars. You don't take a Ferrari to the grocery store, and you don't take a bicycle on a road trip. Same idea here.

**Fast and cheap models** — like Claude Haiku and GPT-4o Mini — are your everyday car. They respond quickly, cost very little, and handle the vast majority of tasks beautifully: quick replies, summarising a document, answering a factual question, drafting a short message. For most personal assistant work, these models are more than good enough.

**Balanced models** — like Claude Sonnet and GPT-4o — sit in the middle. They're noticeably smarter, better at following nuanced instructions, and handle longer, more involved tasks well. When you need a thorough research summary, a well-structured piece of writing, or analysis of something complicated, this is your tier. The cost is higher, but still very reasonable.

**Powerful and expensive models** — like Claude Opus and GPT-4 — are for genuinely hard problems. Complex multi-step reasoning, difficult coding challenges, intricate analysis that requires holding a lot of context at once. Most personal users almost never need these day-to-day. The trap people fall into is starting here because they want "the best", then being shocked by their API bill at the end of the month.

## What Does This Actually Cost?

Real monthly costs depend entirely on how you use your assistant, but here are honest estimates for personal use:

Light use — a few conversations a day, some automations running in the background — comes in around **$1 to $3 a month** when you're using a fast model as your default.

Moderate use — regular conversations, daily scheduled tasks, a handful of integrations actively running — lands around **$5 to $15 a month**.

Heavy use — many automations, long documents, frequent back-and-forth conversations, some Sonnet-level tasks mixed in — sits around **$20 to $50 a month**.

The moment you make Opus or GPT-4 your default model, those numbers jump dramatically. A single long Opus conversation can cost what an entire day of Haiku conversations would.

## The Smart Approach

Start with Claude Haiku as your default. This alone will cover 80% of what you actually need from a personal assistant. Reserve Claude Sonnet for specific tasks where you want more depth — research, longer writing, complex scheduling logic. Avoid Opus or GPT-4 entirely until you have a clear reason to use them.

OpenClaw makes this easy because you can set routing rules: Haiku handles everything by default, but when you ask for something that needs more horsepower, you can either say so explicitly or set up a rule that escalates automatically.

## Common Mistakes

The most common mistake is picking the most powerful model because it feels safer. It's not — it's just more expensive. A Haiku model following a clear instruction will outperform an Opus model following a vague one every single time. Your instructions matter far more than which model you pick.

Another mistake is never checking what you're actually spending. Set a budget alert in your API provider's dashboard and look at it at the end of your first month. Most people are pleasantly surprised. Some aren't, and they quickly learn to adjust.

Don't mix up "capable" with "necessary". A fast model summarising your emails perfectly is better than an expensive model doing the same thing at ten times the cost.
