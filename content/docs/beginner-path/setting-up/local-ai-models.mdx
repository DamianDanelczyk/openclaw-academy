When most people think about AI, they picture something like ChatGPT — you send a message over the internet and an answer comes back from a server somewhere. That's how cloud AI models work, and they're fantastic. But there's another option that not enough people know about: running an AI model directly on your own computer.

This page explains what local models are, when they make sense, and — more importantly — the one principle that determines whether your AI assistant is actually useful or just frustrating.

## What Are Local AI Models?

A local AI model is exactly what it sounds like: an AI that runs on your machine, without sending anything to an external server. Tools like [Ollama](https://ollama.com) make this surprisingly easy — you install the tool, download a model, and it runs entirely on your hardware.

The appeal is clear. Everything stays private. There are no API costs. You can use it offline on a plane or somewhere without internet. For certain tasks, that's genuinely valuable.

The trade-off is capability. Local models are improving fast, but they're generally slower and less capable than the best cloud models. A conversation that Claude handles with ease might leave a smaller local model confused or producing low-quality output. The models that are good enough to be genuinely useful also tend to require decent hardware — ideally a machine with a reasonable GPU.

## When Local Models Make Sense

Local models are a solid choice when the task is simple and privacy matters. Summarising a local document you don't want leaving your machine. Classifying a list of items. Answering basic questions about a file. Drafting a short piece of text. These are well within what good local models handle reliably.

They're also a good fit if you're experimenting and don't want to spend any money while you're still learning how to use your assistant effectively.

Where they struggle: complex reasoning, nuanced writing, research across multiple topics, or anything that requires understanding long, layered context. For those jobs, cloud models are worth the cost — and as you'll see on the [Free & Paid AI Model Options](/en/docs/beginner-path/setting-up/free-and-paid-models) page, the cost is much lower than most people expect.

## The Golden Rule

This is the single most important thing to understand about working with AI, whether you're using a local model or the most powerful cloud model available.

**Tell the AI what you want, not how to do it.**

That sounds simple, but it runs counter to how most people first think about giving instructions. When we try to control things, we tend to over-specify the process. With AI, that usually backfires.

Here's a concrete example. Suppose you want to research competitors for a business idea. A lot of people start by trying to write out every step:

*"Search for companies in the X space, then for each one visit their website, then extract the pricing information, then format it as a table with columns for name, price, and features."*

That kind of instruction is exhausting to write, easy to get wrong, and often produces worse results than it should. You're essentially trying to do the AI's job for it.

The better version is to describe the outcome:

*"I'm thinking about starting a business that does X. Can you find me the five most relevant competitors, and give me a brief summary of what each one does and roughly what they charge?"*

Same goal. Much simpler to express. The AI figures out how to get there.

This matters especially when you're just starting out, because over-specifying is the most common reason beginners get disappointing results. They assume the AI needs hand-holding at every step. It doesn't. It needs a clear destination.

## A Note on Mixing Local and Cloud

One of the things that makes OpenClaw flexible is that you can use different models for different jobs. You might use a local model for quick, private tasks and switch to Claude for anything that needs more horsepower. You don't have to pick one and stick with it.

As you get more comfortable with your setup, you'll develop a natural intuition for which tool fits which task. For now, don't overthink it. Pick one model to start with, use it, and adjust from there.
