---
title: Build Your AI Business Brain
description: How to set up OpenClaw as a persistent business intelligence assistant that knows your company, your clients, and your goals.
---

Every time most people use AI, they start the same way: explaining who they are, what their business does, who their customers are, and what they're trying to achieve. It's exhausting. You're repeating yourself to a tool that forgets you the moment you close the tab.

The "business brain" approach is different. You build a knowledge base that your agent always has access to, so you never have to re-explain your context again. Instead of briefing your assistant every time, you just ask it things — and it already knows the background.

## What Goes Into Your Business Brain

The foundation is a set of workspace files that describe your business in the detail you'd give to a new employee on their first day. Think about what that person would need to know to be genuinely useful without constant supervision.

Your company description is the obvious starting point. A few paragraphs about what you do, who you serve, what problem you solve, and how you're different from the alternatives. Write it the way you'd explain it to someone at a dinner party — clear and human, not like a press release.

Your services and pricing deserve their own document. Be specific. If you offer three tiers, list what each one includes and what it costs. If your pricing varies by client size or project scope, explain how that works. Your agent will use this whenever you ask it to draft proposals, respond to enquiries, or think through how to pitch a potential client.

Your current clients and active projects are worth documenting too. Nothing sensitive — just names, what they hired you for, and any important context. When you ask your agent to "draft a follow-up email for the Acme project," it should already know what Acme does, what stage the project is at, and what the relationship is like.

Your goals for the current month and quarter give your agent a frame of reference for everything it helps you with. If your goal this quarter is to land three new enterprise clients, your agent can factor that in when you ask for help with outreach, proposals, or prioritisation.

Brand voice is often overlooked but genuinely changes the quality of anything your agent writes for you. Give it examples. Show it a piece of your writing that felt right and say "this is the tone I'm going for." Tell it what to avoid — corporate language, passive voice, excessive enthusiasm, whatever doesn't sound like you. The more specific you are, the more your agent's output will actually sound like it came from you.

## How to Set It Up

Create a folder on your computer for your workspace files — or use one you already have. Add it to OpenClaw's workspace settings in the dashboard at [http://127.0.0.1:18789](http://127.0.0.1:18789). Then start dropping in documents.

You don't need to format them in any special way. Plain text files work perfectly. Give each file a clear name so your agent knows what it's looking at: `company-overview.txt`, `services-and-pricing.txt`, `current-clients.txt`, `brand-voice.txt`. Simple names, simple structure.

Once they're in place, test it. Ask your agent something that requires that knowledge: "Draft a proposal for a restaurant owner who wants help with their social media." It should come back with something that mentions your actual services and your actual pricing — without you saying a word about either.

If it doesn't quite land right, look at the file it should have used. Often the issue is that the information is too vague or structured in a confusing way. Rewrite it the way you'd explain it out loud, and try again.

## Keeping It Current

The business brain is only as good as the information in it. Make it a habit to update your workspace files when things change. New service offering? Update the pricing file. A project wraps up? Update the client list. Goals shift? Rewrite the goals document.

Some people do a quick review of their workspace files every month — just a five-minute scan to make sure nothing is out of date. That small habit keeps your agent grounded in your actual reality rather than a version of your business that existed six months ago.

## What This Unlocks

Once your business brain is in place, the quality and relevance of everything your agent produces for you improves across the board. Emails sound like you sent them. Proposals match your actual service structure. Research tasks come back framed in the context of your actual business. Your agent stops being a generic AI tool and starts being something that actually understands your work.

That shift — from generic to contextual — is one of the most meaningful upgrades you can make to how you use OpenClaw.
