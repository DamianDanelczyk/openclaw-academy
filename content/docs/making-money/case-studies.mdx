# Case Studies

Theory is great. Numbers are better. Here are three real-world examples of OpenClaw-powered AI agent deployments that delivered measurable, meaningful results for the businesses that invested in them.

Each case study follows the same structure: the challenge the business faced, the solution that was built, how it was implemented, and the hard numbers that resulted. Use these as inspiration for your own client conversations -- and as proof points when prospects ask, "Does this actually work?"

## Case Study 1: Redwood Realty Group

**Industry:** Residential Real Estate
**Package:** Professional ($2,000/month)
**Timeline:** 6 months

### The Challenge

Redwood Realty Group is a mid-sized real estate brokerage with 14 agents serving the greater Portland, Oregon metro area. Their lead problem wasn't volume -- they were getting 200-300 website inquiries per month. The problem was speed and consistency.

When a prospective buyer or seller submitted an inquiry through their website, the lead was routed to whichever agent was "on call" that day. During business hours, average response time was 47 minutes. After hours and on weekends -- when 58% of their inquiries came in -- the average jumped to 9.5 hours. Some leads never got a response at all.

Industry data is clear: the first agent to respond to a real estate inquiry wins the client 78% of the time. Redwood was leaving an enormous amount of business on the table, and they knew it. They'd tried hiring a virtual assistant to handle after-hours leads, but the quality and consistency were poor, and the cost was $2,800/month with limited coverage.

Their monthly revenue from online leads averaged **$1,200/month** in closed commissions attributable to web inquiries. For a brokerage generating 250+ monthly inquiries, that number was embarrassingly low.

### The Solution

Two OpenClaw AI agents were deployed for Redwood:

**Agent 1: Lead Qualification Bot ("Robin")**
Embedded on the Redwood website and connected to their Facebook page. Robin engages every inquiry within seconds, 24 hours a day. It asks qualifying questions -- budget range, preferred neighborhoods, timeline for buying or selling, pre-approval status -- and categorizes leads as Hot, Warm, or Cold based on the responses.

Hot leads get an immediate text notification sent to the most relevant agent (matched by neighborhood specialty). Warm leads are enrolled in an automated nurture sequence. Cold leads receive helpful resources and are logged in the CRM for future follow-up.

**Agent 2: Property Information Assistant ("Scout")**
Connected to Redwood's listing database, Scout answers specific questions about properties: square footage, year built, school districts, HOA fees, and recent comparable sales. When a prospect asks about a property Scout doesn't have data on, it offers to schedule a showing with the listing agent.

### Implementation

**Week 1:** Discovery session with the brokerage owner and two senior agents. Mapped the entire lead lifecycle from inquiry to closing. Identified the 30 most common questions prospects ask.

**Week 2:** Built Robin's qualification flow and connected it to the brokerage's CRM (Follow Up Boss). Created Scout's knowledge base from 150 active listings. Configured text notifications for hot leads.

**Week 3:** Internal testing with 5 agents role-playing as prospects. Refined Robin's qualifying questions based on agent feedback. Added 40 more Q&A pairs to Scout's knowledge base.

**Week 4:** Soft launch with Robin handling 50% of web traffic. Monitored every conversation. Made daily adjustments to improve response quality.

**Week 5-6:** Full launch across all channels. Training session for all 14 agents on handling escalations and reading lead scores.

### Results (After 6 Months)

| Metric | Before | After | Change |
|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Average response time (all hours) | 4 hrs 12 min | 28 seconds | -99.8% |
| After-hours response time | 9.5 hours | 28 seconds | -99.9% |
| Lead-to-showing conversion rate | 3.2% | 11.7% | +266% |
| Monthly web-attributed revenue | $1,200 | $4,800 | +300% |
| Agent time spent on unqualified leads | ~20 hrs/week | ~5 hrs/week | -75% |
| Client satisfaction (post-showing survey) | 3.6/5 | 4.4/5 | +22% |

The $4,800/month in web-attributed revenue represents a 4x increase from the $1,200 baseline. The brokerage owner estimated that Robin and Scout together replaced the equivalent of 1.5 full-time admin staff while delivering faster, more consistent service.

**ROI:** At a cost of $2,000/month (plus a $1,500 setup fee), the agents generated an additional $3,600/month in revenue -- a 180% monthly return on investment. The setup fee was recouped in the first month.

## Case Study 2: ThreadLine Apparel

**Industry:** E-Commerce (Direct-to-Consumer Fashion)
**Package:** Professional ($2,000/month)
**Timeline:** 4 months

### The Challenge

ThreadLine Apparel is a direct-to-consumer clothing brand selling primarily through Shopify, with annual revenue of approximately $1.8 million. Their product line includes 200+ SKUs across basics, seasonal collections, and limited-edition drops.

Their customer support situation was drowning them. Two full-time support reps handled an average of 1,400 tickets per month across email, Instagram DM, and a website chat widget powered by a basic rules-based chatbot that customers universally hated. The chatbot had a 12% resolution rate -- meaning 88% of people who interacted with it still needed a human.

The support team was spending the majority of their time on a handful of repetitive question categories:
- "Where is my order?" (34% of all tickets)
- "What's your return/exchange policy?" (18%)
- Sizing questions (15%)
- Product availability and restock inquiries (11%)
- Discount code issues (8%)

That's 86% of their ticket volume coming from five categories of questions with straightforward, documentable answers. The support reps were burned out, response times had ballooned to 18 hours on average, and customer complaints on social media were becoming a brand issue.

### The Solution

A single OpenClaw AI agent ("Sage") was deployed across ThreadLine's three support channels: website chat, email, and Instagram DM.

Sage was configured with deep knowledge of ThreadLine's product catalog, policies, shipping carriers, and brand voice. The brand voice piece was particularly important -- ThreadLine has a casual, slightly irreverent tone, and Sage was tuned to match it. Responses feel like texting with a knowledgeable friend, not interacting with corporate support.

Key capabilities:
- **Order tracking:** Integrated with Shopify and ShipStation APIs to pull real-time order status and tracking information.
- **Return/exchange initiation:** Sage can walk customers through the return process, generate return labels, and create exchange orders for different sizes.
- **Sizing guidance:** Trained on ThreadLine's detailed fit guide with recommendations based on customer height, weight, and preferences.
- **Inventory lookup:** Real-time stock checks with the ability to join waitlists for out-of-stock items.
- **Discount troubleshooting:** Validates discount codes, explains eligibility requirements, and can apply manual discounts for edge cases (within pre-approved rules).

### Implementation

**Week 1-2:** Cataloged all 200+ SKUs with sizing data, descriptions, and care instructions. Documented all policies. Analyzed 500 historical support conversations to identify language patterns and edge cases.

**Week 3:** Built and tested Sage across all three channels. Integrated with Shopify, ShipStation, and ThreadLine's returns platform (Loop Returns).

**Week 4:** Launched in "shadow mode" -- Sage drafted responses that human reps reviewed before sending. This caught calibration issues and built the team's trust in the AI.

**Week 5:** Full launch with human escalation for any conversation Sage flagged as uncertain. Monitored daily for the first two weeks.

### Results (After 4 Months)

| Metric | Before | After | Change |
|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Monthly support tickets | 1,400 | 840 | -40% |
| Tickets requiring human response | 1,400 | 420 | -70% |
| Average first response time | 18 hours | 45 seconds | -99.9% |
| Customer satisfaction (CSAT) | 3.1/5 | 4.3/5 | +39% |
| Support staff hours/week | 80 hrs (2 FTEs) | 35 hrs | -56% |
| Negative social media mentions (support-related) | ~12/month | ~2/month | -83% |

The 40% reduction in total support tickets was partly driven by Sage resolving issues so quickly that customers didn't need to follow up -- previously, 15-20% of tickets were follow-ups on unanswered initial inquiries.

ThreadLine didn't lay off either support rep. Instead, they redirected the freed-up time toward proactive customer outreach, VIP customer management, and product feedback collection -- work that directly contributes to retention and repeat purchases. One rep estimated that she now spends 60% of her time on "work that actually matters" versus the 10% before Sage.

**ROI:** The two support reps' combined salary and benefits cost ThreadLine approximately $8,500/month. While the reps weren't eliminated, the 56% reduction in support hours freed up the equivalent of $4,760/month in labor capacity -- redirected toward revenue-generating activities. Against a $2,000/month agent cost, the math works decisively in ThreadLine's favor.

## Case Study 3: Catalyst Marketing Co.

**Industry:** Marketing Consulting
**Package:** Starter ($500/month) -- later upgraded to Professional ($2,000/month)
**Timeline:** 8 months

### The Challenge

Jenna Maris runs Catalyst Marketing Co. as a solo marketing consultant specializing in brand strategy and content marketing for B2B SaaS companies. She's excellent at what she does, which created a classic solo consultant problem: she was at capacity with 4 clients and couldn't take on more without quality suffering.

The bottleneck wasn't strategy work -- that's the high-value thinking that clients pay premium rates for. The bottleneck was everything around it:
- Responding to client Slack messages and emails (estimated 2 hours/day)
- Drafting initial versions of blog posts, social media copy, and email sequences (estimated 10 hours/week)
- Compiling weekly and monthly performance reports (estimated 3 hours/week per client)
- Scheduling and managing meetings (estimated 3 hours/week)

Jenna was working 55-60 hours per week and turning away referrals. At $5,000/month per client, her revenue was capped at $20,000/month -- solid income, but with no room for growth and an unsustainable workload.

### The Solution

The engagement started small. Jenna initially signed up for the Starter tier with a single AI agent ("Muse") focused on content drafting.

**Phase 1 -- Content Drafting Agent (Months 1-3)**
Muse was trained on Jenna's writing style, her clients' brand guidelines, and a library of her best-performing content. It drafts initial versions of blog posts, LinkedIn posts, email sequences, and ad copy. Jenna reviews and refines every draft -- but starting from a solid first draft instead of a blank page cut her content production time by roughly 60%.

**Phase 2 -- Reporting Agent (Months 4-5, upgraded to Professional)**
A second agent ("Metric") was added to automate performance reporting. Metric pulls data from Google Analytics, HubSpot, and LinkedIn, generates a formatted weekly summary with key metrics and trend analysis, and produces a monthly deck with visualizations. Jenna reviews each report in 15 minutes instead of spending 3 hours building it from scratch.

**Phase 3 -- Client Communication Agent (Months 6-8)**
A third agent ("Bridge") was deployed to handle routine client communications. Bridge monitors Jenna's client Slack channels and email, drafts responses to routine questions (project timelines, asset requests, meeting scheduling), and flags anything requiring Jenna's personal attention. Jenna reviews and sends Bridge's drafts with minor edits, reducing her communication overhead by about 70%.

### Implementation

This was a gradual rollout, which is often the smartest approach for solo consultants.

**Month 1:** Trained Muse on Jenna's content library (50+ blog posts, 200+ social posts, 30+ email sequences). Established her brand voice profile and client-specific style guides.

**Month 2-3:** Iterated on content quality. Jenna provided detailed feedback on every draft for the first month, which steadily improved Muse's output. By month 3, roughly 40% of Muse's drafts needed only light editing.

**Month 4:** Added Metric. Connected data sources. Built report templates matching Jenna's existing format so clients wouldn't notice any change in deliverable quality.

**Month 6:** Added Bridge. This required the most careful configuration since it involved client-facing communication. Started with Bridge handling only scheduling and asset requests, then gradually expanded its scope.

### Results (After 8 Months)

| Metric | Before | After | Change |
|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Active clients | 4 | 12 | +200% (3x capacity) |
| Monthly revenue | $20,000 | $55,000 | +175% |
| Weekly working hours | 55-60 hrs | 40-45 hrs | -25% |
| Time on content drafting | 10 hrs/week | 4 hrs/week | -60% |
| Time on reporting | 12 hrs/week (all clients) | 3 hrs/week | -75% |
| Time on routine communication | 10 hrs/week | 3 hrs/week | -70% |

The headline number: Jenna went from 4 clients to 12 -- a 3x increase in capacity -- while actually working fewer hours per week. Her monthly revenue grew from $20,000 to $55,000 (some of the new clients are on smaller retainers, which explains why revenue didn't triple perfectly).

More importantly, Jenna is now focused almost exclusively on strategic work -- the part she loves and the part clients value most. The administrative and production tasks that were eating 60% of her time now consume about 20%.

Jenna has since hired a junior consultant and is considering building a small agency -- a trajectory that wasn't remotely possible before her AI agents freed up her capacity.

**ROI:** Jenna's agent cost escalated from $500/month to $2,000/month as she upgraded and added capabilities. Against an incremental $35,000/month in revenue, the ROI speaks for itself. Even accounting for the additional costs of serving more clients (tools, subcontractors, etc.), her profit margin improved from approximately 70% to 78%.
