# Pricing Your AI Agent Services

Pricing is where most new agency owners tie themselves in knots. They either price too low out of insecurity and end up resenting their clients, or they pull a number out of thin air and can't justify it when someone pushes back.

Neither approach works. What does work is a structured methodology that accounts for your costs, your market, the value you deliver, and where you want your business to go. Let's build that together.

## The Pricing Calculator Methodology

Before you set any prices, you need to understand your actual cost to deliver. Not roughly. Precisely.

### Step 1: Calculate Your Base Costs

Start with what it costs you to deliver a single client engagement.

**Platform Costs**
What do you pay OpenClaw (or any other tools) per client? This might be a flat platform fee, a per-agent cost, or a usage-based charge. Write down the monthly cost per client.

**Your Time**
Be honest here. How many hours does it take you to set up a new client? How many hours per month do you spend on ongoing maintenance and optimization? Track this for your first few clients -- your estimates will probably be wrong.

Assign yourself an hourly rate. If you're not sure what yours should be, start with what you'd need to earn to make this your full-time income. Take your target annual income, divide by 2,000 (roughly 50 weeks times 40 hours), and that's your baseline hourly rate.

Example: $100,000 annual target / 2,000 hours = $50/hour

**Software and Tools**
Communication tools, project management, reporting software, CRM -- add up the per-client portion of your monthly tool costs.

**Overhead**
Internet, co-working space, insurance, accounting -- divide your total monthly overhead by your current or projected number of clients.

### Step 2: Set Your Floor Price

Add up all the costs from Step 1. This is your floor -- the absolute minimum you can charge and still break even. You should never price below this number.

Example calculation for a Professional-tier client:
- OpenClaw platform: $50/month
- Your time (6 hours/month x $50/hour): $300/month
- Tools (pro-rated): $25/month
- Overhead (pro-rated): $75/month
- **Total cost: $450/month**

### Step 3: Apply Your Margin

Your margin is what turns a service into a business. For professional services, a healthy margin is 50-70%. Apply it to your floor price.

- $450 cost x 2.5 (60% margin) = **$1,125/month minimum**
- $450 cost x 3.0 (67% margin) = **$1,350/month minimum**

Notice how the Professional tier at $2,000/month starts to make a lot of sense? That's a healthy margin that gives you room to invest in growth, handle the occasional difficult client, and not burn out.

### Step 4: Validate Against Value

Now check your price against the value you're delivering. If your Professional client is saving $4,000/month in staff time or generating $3,000/month in additional revenue from captured leads, then $2,000/month is an easy yes for them. Your price should typically be 20-40% of the measurable value you deliver.

If the value doesn't justify the price, either find higher-value use cases or adjust your pricing. Don't just discount -- reframe.

## Competitive Analysis Framework

You don't set prices in a vacuum. Understanding what the market looks like helps you position yourself effectively. But here's the key: you're not trying to be the cheapest. You're trying to be appropriately priced for the value and experience you offer.

### How to Research Competitors

**Direct Competitors** -- Other AI agent agencies or freelancers serving your target market. Look for their pricing on their websites, in case studies, or on freelancing platforms.

**Adjacent Competitors** -- Virtual assistant services, chatbot companies, marketing automation agencies. These aren't identical to what you offer, but your prospects might compare you to them.

**Alternative Solutions** -- What's the client's alternative to hiring you? Usually it's hiring a full-time employee, keeping things manual, or using a DIY chatbot builder. Understanding these costs frames your value.

### Building Your Comparison Matrix

| Factor | You | Competitor A | Competitor B | DIY Solution |
|--------|-----|-------------|-------------|--------------|
| Monthly cost | $2,000 | $1,500 | $3,500 | $99 + time |
| Setup time | 1-2 weeks | 3-4 weeks | 2-3 weeks | Ongoing |
| Customization | High | Medium | High | Low |
| Ongoing support | Included | Extra cost | Included | None |
| Industry expertise | Deep | General | General | None |
| Response quality | High | Medium | High | Low |

This matrix isn't for your prospects -- it's for you. It shows you where you're strong, where you're weak, and where you have room to differentiate. If everyone in your market charges $1,500-$2,000 for a similar service, pricing at $2,000 with better support and industry expertise is a winning position. Pricing at $800 just signals insecurity.

## Value-Based Pricing Strategies

Cost-plus pricing (the calculator methodology above) gives you a floor. Value-based pricing gives you a ceiling. The goal is to move your actual prices closer to that ceiling over time.

### The ROI Conversation

During discovery calls, always quantify the client's current pain in dollar terms. This is your leverage for value-based pricing.

**The formula is simple:**
- "How many leads do you miss per month due to slow response times?" (e.g., 20)
- "What's the average value of a closed deal?" (e.g., $5,000)
- "What's your typical close rate?" (e.g., 10%)
- Missed revenue = 20 leads x $5,000 x 10% = **$10,000/month**

If your agent captures even half of those missed leads, you're adding $5,000/month in potential revenue. Suddenly, charging $2,000/month isn't expensive -- it's a 150% return on investment.

Document this math in your proposal. Make the ROI case explicitly, with the client's own numbers. When they see their specific dollars at stake, price resistance melts away.

### Pricing by Client Size, Not by Deliverable

A common mistake is pricing the same for every client regardless of their size. A 50-person company gets enormously more value from an AI agent than a 3-person shop, even if the technical setup is identical. Your pricing should reflect that.

This doesn't mean you need a complex pricing formula. It means your tiers naturally align with business size:
- Starter ($500/mo) targets sole proprietors and micro-businesses
- Professional ($2,000/mo) targets established small businesses with teams
- Enterprise ($5,000+/mo) targets larger operations where the stakes are higher

The same agent configuration might cost you the same to build, but the value it delivers -- and therefore the price it commands -- scales with the client's business.

### Anchor Pricing

When presenting your tiers, always show the Enterprise tier first. This is the anchor. When a prospect sees $5,000+/month first, the Professional tier at $2,000/month feels reasonable by comparison. If you start with Starter at $500/month and work up, everything after feels expensive.

In proposals, structure your pricing section like this:

1. Enterprise at $5,000+/month (the anchor)
2. Professional at $2,000/month (the recommended option -- mark it as such)
3. Starter at $500/month (the entry point)

### Packaging Add-Ons

Not everything needs to fit in a tier. Certain high-value services work better as add-ons that any client can purchase regardless of their tier.

- Additional AI agent: $300-$800/month depending on complexity
- New platform integration: $500 one-time setup + $100/month
- Custom analytics dashboard: $500 one-time setup + $200/month
- Rush setup (delivery in 48 hours vs. 1-2 weeks): 50% surcharge on setup fee
- Training workshop for client's team (beyond initial training): $500/session
- Quarterly strategy deep-dive (for Starter/Professional clients): $750/session

Add-ons increase your average revenue per client without requiring you to redesign your tier structure. They also give clients a sense of control -- they're choosing what they want rather than being told what they get.

## When to Raise Your Prices

This is the question every agency owner struggles with, and the answer is simpler than you think: raise your prices sooner than feels comfortable.

### Signals It's Time

**You're closing more than 80% of proposals.** If almost everyone says yes, you're underpriced. A healthy close rate is 40-60%. Anything higher means you're leaving significant money on the table.

**You're at capacity.** If you can't take on new clients without dropping quality, raise prices. Higher prices with fewer clients means the same revenue with less work -- and better service for the clients you keep.

**Your results are getting stronger.** As you accumulate case studies, testimonials, and experience, your value proposition improves. Your pricing should follow.

**You haven't raised prices in 6+ months.** If you've been delivering consistently and improving your service, a 10-20% increase every 6-12 months is not only reasonable -- it's expected by professional clients.

**The market is moving.** If competitors are charging more than you for comparable or inferior services, the market is telling you something.

### How to Raise Prices

**For new clients:** Just change the numbers on your website and proposals. No announcement needed.

**For existing clients:** Give 60 days notice, explain the new pricing, and emphasize the improvements you've made. Frame it as an investment in better service.

A template:

> Hi [Name],
>
> I wanted to give you a heads-up that starting [date], our Professional tier pricing will be moving from $2,000/month to $2,400/month. This reflects the expanded capabilities we've added over the past year, including [specific improvements].
>
> As a valued client, your new rate won't take effect until [date -- 60 days out], giving you plenty of time to plan. I'm also happy to lock in your current rate for an additional 3 months if you'd like to commit to a 6-month term.
>
> Thanks for continuing to trust us with your business. The results we've been seeing together have been fantastic, and I'm excited about what's ahead.

Most clients will accept a reasonable increase without pushback. The ones who leave over a 10-20% price increase were likely to churn anyway. And the clients who stay are now more profitable, giving you room to serve them even better.
